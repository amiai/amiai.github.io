Redesigning Myagi Home

Project Overview

Team

Scrum team consisted of 3 software engineers, 1 QA engineer, 1 designer, and 1 product manager. Design team consisted of 3 designers, where I led the Home Page Frontend Refactor project as the designer assigned to the scrum team.

Role

As the lead designer for this project, I was responsible for redesigning the Myagi app. This involved conducting user research (interviews, surveys), ideation of UI designs, and handover of designs to engineers. I was also responsible for facilitating feedback workshops while articulating design decisions from a technical and business standpoint to my scrum team and to stakeholders.

What is Myagi?

Myagi is a Retail Sales Enablement platform that lets brand and retailers deliver the right product knowledge to retail sales associates at scale. Through Myagi, both suppliers and their reseller partners can optimize sell-through by improving what we call the Knowledge Supply Chain—the path information takes to get from its start to end, across multiple companies and teams and people. If you've ever had issues with important knowledge not getting to the right place, not getting there in time, not being understood, or easily being forgotten, you probably have a broken Knowledge Supply Chain.

Problem Statement

The Myagi Home page is not being utilised frequently by users due to the lack of clear informational hierarchy displayed to users, and lack of understanding regarding users' needs on the Home page. The Home page has also accumulated technical and design-debt which required critical attention as it is a page that has the most user traffic.

Objectives + Goals

The refactor and redesign of the Home page was a part of the Frontend Refactor project. Our primary objective for this project was to refactor and redesign this page in order to reduce tech and design-debt, and also to improve the usability of the Home page for our users.

As the Home page has the most user traffic, it was imperative for the team to retain the main features that users were familiar with seeing and using. It was important to ensure that the learning curve and user friction was minimal. To achieve this, we decided to release an MVP version first. To figure out requirements for the MVP, we collaborated with PMs to conduct a survey, and we then categorised these requirements by order of priority. This was done so that there was a clear understanding of features that were currently most used by users.

Design Approach

While utilising a Double Diamond design process, our design approach for this project has been depicted in a simplified flow below.

It was important for the team to understand the problems that we needed to solve for, and also our users' needs when interacting with the Home page. After the audits and user research (done in the form of surveys) have been completed, we determine key usability issues based on our findings. During the UI ideation, we try to solve for these issues and attempt to visualise these concepts. Once the concepts have been finalised, we wanted to get feedback via internal stakeholders to ensure that the concepts would not result in potential issues further down the line. Changes and feedback were then documented and implemented before handover. Further information on each stage of this approach will be discussed throughout this case study.

Product Users

For this project, we targeted four user types:

  • Retail Admins
  • Brand Admins
  • Sales Associates
  • Team Managers

These user types were ones that frequent the Myagi app most, and are our main user types. User types have different goals when interacting with the Myagi product. Retail users (Retail Admins, Sales Associates, Team Managers) want to see their performance, progress, find out which content to consume, and to see what they've completed. Brand Admins want to create content, drive engagement, and view analytics.

User Flows

Our team dedicated focused efforts to create detailed user flows as a fundamental step before initiating any refactoring. By mapping out user flows, we aimed to gain a comprehensive understanding of both our product and our users. Through this process, we visualised the pathways users navigate within the product, analysing their interactions, and decision points. The user flows allowed us to comprehend the existing user journey, identify pain points, and recognise areas for potential improvement. We created user flows for each feature/section on the Home page including: Feed, Action Items, and Your Performance.

Survey

Survey Overview

Prior to refactoring and redesigning the Home page, the team found that there was a lack of documentation regarding this page.


As a result, this led to us not knowing:

  • The purpose of features on the Home page
  • How users are using the Home page
  • Which features are important to users

We needed this information to:

  • Decide the hierarchy of information on the page
  • Make improvements
  • Prioritise efforts
  • Avoid disruptions to existing user flows

From this knowledge, we believed that conducting a survey would allow us to gain a better understand of our users' needs on the Myagi Home page. We also wanted to gather intel on how we might be able to improve the Home page as it would help inform our design and product decisions.


Survey Metrics

Using data from Google Analytics, we determined the approximate number of responses we aimed to receive from the survey. As displayed in the screenshot below, the number of Active Users for the pathway “/view/home” within the last 7 days (13th - 19th April 2023) was 8390 users. We aimed to have a 5% response rate which was approximately 420 survey responses.



Survey Distribution

In order to target our different user types, we sent out three separate surveys to gather appropriate intel for each user type. As discussed previously, these were the users we targeted and focused on:

  • Retail Admins
  • Brand Admins
  • Sales Associates
  • Team Managers

Survey Questions

  1. What do you typically use the home page for?
  2. Which features on the home page are most important to you?
  3. Which features on the home page are least important to you?
  4. What pain points do you experience when using the home page?
  5. What improvements would you like to see on the home page?
  6. Was there anything we missed that you want to let us know?

Outcome

The survey was released through Intercom and was displayed to users who were on the Home page. The survey was active for one week.


Survey Results

Due to limited time and resources, the survey was only active for 5 days (27th April - 1st May 2023). We received a total of 155 survey responses, with 89 responses being the highest received from Sales Associates and Team Managers (See image below).

UI Ideation

Using the data we gained from the surveys and audits, we ideated multiple options for the Home page in order to determine and visualise concepts that would solve for the usability issues we discovered during the research phase. Below are two options we ideated which experiments with different layouts for this page.


Figure 4.1 — This option places the feed on a higher visual hierarchy compared to the 'Action Items'/'Tasks' and 'Your Performance' section. This option was mainly targeted towards Brand Admins where majority of them use the Home page to “Click on post content” and/or “Write a post”.


Figure 4.2 — This option places the 'Action Items'/'Tasks' and 'Your Performance' on a higher visual hierarchy compared to the feed. From the survey results, we found that majority of users who responded were Retail Admins, Sales Associates, and Team Managers; And that they all typically use the Home page to view their performance. Hence, we wanted to ideate an option that emphasises that clearly on the Home page for the vast majority of our users.

Feedback Workshop

After a few iterations of UI ideation, the team selected two options to get feedback on. We decided to get feedback via internal stakeholders, however, due to time-zone differences, we opted for an async method of gathering feedback. Using Miro, we included the project context, step-by-step instructions of what to do, and the type of feedback we were looking for. As the Home page was a significant page within our product which garnered alot of user traffic, this feedback workshop ensured that we were able to receive a range of feedback from various departments including: Customer Success, Marketing, Engineering, and Product teams.


Post-Feedback Workshop

The Async Feedback Workshop was open for 1 week before we closed it to retrieve the feedback that was given. As shown in the screenshot below, internal stakeholders placed comments and feedback on Miro. We found that this method of getting feedback was efficient, and allowed internal stakeholders to provide feedback at a time that suits them.



Organising Feedback

After closing the Feedback Workshop, we went through the feedback and organised it by type (Improvement, New Feature, To Consider), Impact, and Effort. Categorising feedback by Impact vs. Effort allowed us to prioritise which feedback we could implement efficiently in the time frame that we had remaining. We targeted and focused on feedback that had a High Impact and Low Effort.

Final

One of the main objectives, which was discussed initially in the MVP requirements, was the need for clear visual hierarchy on the Home page. From the survey results, we found that alot of our users felt that the Home page was “hard to navigate”. As discussed previously in this case study, we also found that majority of users who responded to the survey were Retail Admins, Sales Associates, and Team Managers; And that they all typically use the Home page to view their performance ('Your Performance' section). As a result, the final option that the team selected emphasises the 'Your Performance' section first, with it being located at the top of the page. The feed and the 'Action Items'/'Tasks' follow after in terms of information and visual hierarchy on the page.


Reflection

What do you think was done well?

User research went well as we were able to gather and collect the necessary information needed in order to successfully refactor the Home page. In particular, we conducted a survey via Intercom, which was displayed to users when they arrived on the Home page of the Myagi app. As a result, we were able to gain valuable quantitative and qualitative feedback.


What could have been improved?

During usability testing, we had some users who were not fluent in English. As a result, there was a small language barrier when asking questions, and also when they interacted with the mockups which were all in English. We could definitely improve on this by adding text translations to mockups. However, it is also important to understand that this may not be applicable to every usability test conducted in the case that there are time constraints and limited resources.


What would I do differently if I were to approach the same problem again?

The Myagi Home page had alot of user traffic, which was why it was necessary to ensure that the redesigned page did not cause any additional friction to our users' experience. However, although the refactoring and redesigning of this page was a critical project for the team, there was a lack of usability testing after its release. Hence, if I were to approach a similar project, I would encourage and push for the team to implement clearly defined success metrics. Additionally, I believe it would also be beneficial to have the survey active for longer than the 5 days we had it active for. This was a result of limited time and resources, however, the team would have been able to gain alot more valuable insight if there was more time and opportunity for users to respond to the survey.

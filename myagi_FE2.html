Myagi Frontend Refactor

Project Overview

Team

Scrum team consisted of 3 software engineers, 1 QA engineer, 1 designer, and 1 product manager. Design team consisted of 3 designers, where I led the Frontend Refactor project as the designer assigned to the scrum team.

Role

As the lead designer for this project, I was responsible for redesigning the Myagi app. This involved conducting user research (interviews, surveys), ideation of UI designs, and handover of designs to engineers. I was also responsible for facilitating feedback workshops while articulating design decisions from a technical and business standpoint to my scrum team and to stakeholders.

What is Myagi?

Myagi is a Retail Sales Enablement platform that lets brand and retailers deliver the right product knowledge to retail sales associates at scale. Through Myagi, both suppliers and their reseller partners can optimize sell-through by improving what we call the Knowledge Supply Chain—the path information takes to get from its start to end, across multiple companies and teams and people. If you've ever had issues with important knowledge not getting to the right place, not getting there in time, not being understood, or easily being forgotten, you probably have a broken Knowledge Supply Chain.

Problem Statement

The Myagi Product has grown to a stage where customers are requesting a variety of features that we are unable to deliver efficiently due to the accumulation of technical and design-debt within the product.

Objectives + Goals

Our primary objective for this project was to refactor and redesign the Myagi app's frontend to reduce tech and design-debt. From a design perspective, our goal was to improve the product's overall UX and UI. The Myagi product features 6 key directories accessible via the navigation bar:

  • Home
  • Learning
  • Content
  • People
  • People
  • Analytics
  • Network

The frontend refactor project involved the refactor and redesign of the sections listed above, which are also inclusive of the individual pages within each section.

Design Process

Throughout our design projects, our team embraced the Double Diamond process, a framework that guided our approach from problem identification to solution implementation.

The process consists of four key phases:

  1. Discover
  2. Define
  3. Develop / Define
  4. Deliver

During the 'Discover' phase, we delved into extensive research, engaging in user interviews, market analysis, and comprehensive data collection to gain a deep understanding of the problem at hand. Moving into the 'Define' phase, we distilled and analysed our research, identifying core issues and defining the problem space. With a clear definition in hand, we shifted into the 'Develop' phase, generating a wide array of ideas and solutions. This involved brainstorming, prototyping, and testing concepts to explore various possibilities. Finally, in the 'Deliver' phase, we honed in on the best solutions, refining and implementing them to create the final design before handing over to engineers.

By traversing through divergent and convergent thinking in each phase of the Double Diamond, we ensured a systematic and creative approach, leading to well-informed and user-centred design solutions.

The diagram below depicts a simplified view of the Double Diamond process. An important and significant part of this process is iterating on our designs during the 'Develop' phase. As mentioned, this process involves and focuses on generating a multitude of ideas, which is why it is crucial for us to constantly iterate on designs until we reach a stage where it addressed the problems that we were trying to solve for.

Design Approach

Our design approach varied depending on the context of the page, as each page had its own usability issues which required problem solving with different sets of solutions. However, our design approach would always consist of and begin with looking at the platform as a whole. We took on a holistic approach as the purpose of this project was to refactor and redesign the Myagi product. It was critical that we understood how a component may look and function on two different pages, and that redesigned components were consistent throughout the product.

Prior to the refactoring of the sections and pages, it was also important for the team to consider the learning curve that users would have to face once the refactored pages were released.

Product Users

The Myagi product has a variety of users that rely on our app for a multitude of reasons. Frequently used features within our product are quite dependant on the user type. For example, brands (marketing managers/associates) would be more focused on growing a network of connections via our product. Whereas retailers (sales associates) would be more focused on learning content that they receive from brands in order to train their staff.

Some examples of our users:

  • Brands (Marketing managers/associates)
  • Retailers (Sales asssociates)
  • Account Admins
  • Team Managers
  • Area Managers

User types have different goals when interacting with the Myagi product. Retail users (Retail Admins, Sales Associates, Team Managers) want to see their performance, progress, find out which content to consume, and to see what they've completed. Brand Admins want to create content, drive engagement, and view analytics.

User, Business, and Technical Needs

One of the main business goals was decreasing churn rate, and to do so, the product team prioritised the refactoring project as a pathway to improving our customers' experience when interacting with our product. It was important for us to understand both user needs and business goals in order to achieve this objective. However, due to the nature of this project - Refactoring the product to reduce tech and design-debt, it was also essential to understand the technical constraints that came with this project. For example, the team had to be aware that we were unable to touch the backend, or change any significant feature behaviour as we were only focused on refactoring the frontend.

User Flows

Our team dedicated focused efforts to create detailed user flows as a fundamental step before initiating any refactoring. By mapping out user flows, we aimed to gain a comprehensive understanding of both our product and our users. Through this process, we visualised the pathways users navigate within the product, analysing their interactions, and decision points. The user flows allowed us to comprehend the existing user journey, identify pain points, and recognise areas for potential improvement.


Information Architecture

It was important that we had a clear comprehension of the current information architecture to ensure that changes made will not disrupt our customers' current experience and knowledge of where expected information is located. We analysed and mapped out the product's information architecture for each section, including the pages within. This in-depth understanding provided a solid foundation for our refactoring endeavours, enabling us to strategically address specific user needs and restructure the product in a way that enhanced usability and satisfaction.


Heuristic Evaluation

Our team conducted heuristic evaluations and comprehensive audits of our application to delve deep into its usability and functionality. Through this process, we assessed the product against established usability principles and best practices. This evaluation was instrumental in identifying a spectrum of user experience issues and design inconsistencies that might cause confusion or hinder user interactions.

By analysing these findings, we were able to identify critical pain points and areas in need of improvement within the application. The insights garnered from this evaluation served as a compass guiding our subsequent refactoring project. With a clear understanding of these identified pain points, we strategically channelled our efforts to address and rectify these issues, ensuring that the changes were targeted precisely to enhance the overall user experience and streamline the app's functionality.


Figure 3 — We used a key legend to assign tags for each heuristic to help determine our priorities, and to understand where we needed to focus our efforts on.

Quantitative Research

To help gain insight and understanding as to how our users interact with the Myagi product, we used Mouseflow to track user behaviour on various pages of the app. Through the use of Mouseflow, the team was able to analyse clicks, movements, scrolls, navigation patterns, and also the level of friction users may have on a particular page. This data-driven approach provided quantitative data that was crucial in shaping informed design decisions and allowed the team to identify pain points, areas of high engagement, and potential usability issues.

Reflection

What do you think was done well?

Cross-functional collaboration with various teams including: Engineering, Product, Customer Success, and Marketing went well. Async feedback workshops were conducted to gain feedback, suggestions, and ideas from teams and stakeholders. This was a large project which involved the redesign of the entire Myagi app, which meant that the impact it would have on the business is quite significant. With this knowledge in mind, cross-functional collaboration was a crucial aspect in ensuring the app meets user, business, and technical needs.


What could have been improved?

Due to time and resource constraints, there was a lack of data and testing after refactored pages were released to customers. The product team mainly relied on feedback that the customer success team received during customer calls. Moreover, there were occasional feature requests that were highly prioritised, which meant that the team had to compromise on the quality of released pages, and focus on quick delivery instead.


What would I do differently if I were to approach the same problem again?

While the initial Myagi app had user tracking and analytics via Mouseflow, the newly refactored pages did not have that. Hence, I would encourage and push for the team to integrate that so that we would be able to analyse the performance of the refactored pages. Alternatively, I would also encourage for usability testing post-release of refactored pages.
